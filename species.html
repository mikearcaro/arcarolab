The importance of animal research

Animal research underpins most of the defining advances in modern medicine. Penicillin's clinical potential was first confirmed in mice. The entire childhood vaccine schedule, from polio to COVID-19, required animal safety testing before human trials. Statins and beta blockers were both developed in animal models; statins alone prevent an estimated 200,000 deaths annually in the United States. The understanding of how neurons fire came from Hodgkin and Huxley's experiments in squid; the genetic basis of cancer from studies in fruit flies, mice, and retroviruses. Within vision science, work in cats and monkeys produced the foundational account of visual cortex organization, establishing cortical columns, critical periods, and experience-dependent plasticity as principles that still define the field.

Our lab applies that same logic. Humans, rhesus macaques, and tree shrews differ substantially in brain size, developmental timeline, and foveal specialization, yet all three share the basic mammalian blueprint for visual cortex organization. Studying the same questions across these species lets us separate what is universal from what is lineage-specific. When a principle holds across all three we can be confident it reflects something fundamental about how visual systems are built. When a feature differs we gain leverage on the forces that shaped it.
